Summary

Origin’s Design of Experiments app allows users to design experiments, fit models to collected data, and identify the best factor settings to optimize responses.
In this tutorial, we will explore two types of screening designs: the Plackett-Burman design and the Definitive Screening Design (DSD).
During the initial design stage, investigators often identify many factors that may influence the response variable. In such cases, it may be impractical to run a full or fractional factorial design. Instead, a screening design can be used to identify the factors that have the most significant effects.
The Plackett-Burman design is a cost-effective approach used to identify the most important main effects. This design is appropriate if you can reasonably assume that:

  • Two-way interactions are negligible.
  • Quadratic (squared) terms are negligible, implying that any curvature in the response is minimal.


If these assumptions do not hold, a Definitive Screening Design may be more suitable. DSDs require only a small number of experimental runs, but they use three levels for continuous factors, allowing the detection of curvature in the response. Despite the limited number of runs, this design can estimate main effects, as well as some interaction and quadratic terms.


Example where the Plackett-Burman design is suitable

A company is producing a new gluten-free bread and has identified eleven factors that may impact Loaf Volume:

  1. Amount of rice flour, tapioca starch, xantham gum, yeast, water, and oil used
  2. The mixing time, proofing time, and baking time
  3. The proofing temperature and baking temperature

The company is interested in conducting a screening experiment to identify the most important main effects. They believe that most of the factors act independently of each other. Any interaction effects will be investigated later, using a fractional or full fractional design.


Create Design

  1. Select and click on the Design of Experiments app on in the Stats in the Apps Gallery
  2. Click on Create Design and select Factorial Design in the pop-up list.
  3. In the Factorial Design dialog, select Plackett-Burman as Factorial Type. Click Display Available Designs button to explore the number of runs that are required using a Plackett-Burman Design. With 11 factors, at least 12 runs are required.
